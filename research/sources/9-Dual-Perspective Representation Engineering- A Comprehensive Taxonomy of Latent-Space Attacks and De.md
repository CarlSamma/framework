Dual-Perspective Representation Engineering: A Comprehensive Taxonomy of Latent-Space Attacks and Defenses in Large Language Models
Deep neural networks have achieved unprecedented success in natural language understanding and instruction compliance, yet their internal activation spaces and representational mechanics remain highly complex and difficult to interpret.[1, 2] While traditional interpretability research has focused primarily on mechanistic interpretability—attempting to reverse-engineer individual neurons, circuits, and attention heads—the emerging paradigm of Representation Engineering (RepE) offers a top-down alternative.[1, 2, 3] Drawing inspiration from cognitive neuroscience, RepE treats high-dimensional, population-level representations as the fundamental unit of analysis.[1, 2, 4] This view posits that cognition is implemented by patterns of activity across neural populations, mapping abstract concepts to continuous trajectories within the model's high-dimensional representation space.[1, 2]
This analytical framework is divided into representation reading and representation control.[1, 2] Representation reading locates latent concepts (such as honesty, utility, morality, or harm) by projecting neural activations onto lower-dimensional subspaces.[1, 2] Representation control subsequently manipulates these activations during inference to steer model outputs along desired behavioral axes without altering the underlying static weights.[5, 6, 7] Mathematically, standard representation control shifts the activation state h^{(l)} at a target layer l using a steering vector v_{\text{steering}} scaled by an injection coefficient c [6, 8]:
h_{\text{modified}}^{(l)} = h^{(l)} + c \cdot v_{\text{steering}}
While this direct intervention bypasses the limitations of prompt engineering and the high costs of weight fine-tuning, it also creates a complex, dual-use security landscape.[6, 8, 9] This report details the dual-perspective taxonomy of Representation Engineering, classifying the security vulnerabilities exploited by the latent-space attacker (Perspective B) and the countermeasures developed by the latent-space defender (Perspective A).
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Conceptual Foundations of Latent-Space Dynamics
To understand the mechanics of latent-space attacks and defenses, it is necessary to examine the underlying principles governing representation formation and stability.[3, 10]
The Input-Space Linearity Hypothesis posits that concept-aligned directions originate directly within the model's input embedding space and are steadily maintained with increasing transformer depth.[3] Deep networks progressively distill these linear representations along Spectral Principal Paths (SPP), where multimodal and unimodal representations are preserved and transformed along the principal eigenvector.[3] This stable structure explains why low-rank representation adaptations and linear difference-in-means vectors can consistently steer high-level behaviors.[2, 3, 11] During the generation process, early layers display high representational plasticity, while middle and late layers exhibit a responsive critical window where targeted interventions yield stable behavioral shifts.[3]
However, activation steering reveals a fundamental divergence between internal representations and textual prompts, formalized as the Surjectivity Problem.[10] This problem addresses whether steered activation states can be replicated by any natural textual sequence.[10] Analysis indicates that activation steering pushes the residual stream off the manifold of states reachable through discrete token prompting.[10] Almost surely, no prompt can reproduce the internal hidden states induced by white-box activation steering.[10] This establishes a formal mathematical separation between white-box steerability and black-box prompting, meaning that steered behavior is fundamentally distinct from prompt-based control.[10] This separation allows attackers to bypass prompt-layer guardrails entirely, while also enabling defenders to enforce safety constraints to a degree unachievable through standard instruction fine-tuning.[9, 10]

Perspective B: Classification of Representation-Level Attacks
From the attacker's perspective, representation engineering and activation steering represent highly effective methods for bypassing alignment guardrails, extracting training data, and embedding covert backdoors.[9, 12, 13] These exploits are categorized into three distinct classes of latent-space attacks.
Type I: Direct Hidden-State Interventions (Inference-Time Attacks)
Direct hidden-state interventions exploit the model's latent vulnerabilities at inference time by injecting or removing activation vectors.[9, 12, 14]
The vulnerability of the latent space to direct activation perturbations is demonstrated by the "rogue scalpel" phenomenon, where benign or random activation adjustments systematically compromise model alignment.[12, 14, 15] Sampling vectors uniformly from a unit-norm sphere S^{d-1} and injecting them as random noise into the middle layers of a model during inference breaks refusal mechanisms, raising the probability of compliance with harmful requests from 0% to between 1% and 27%.[12, 15, 16] This vulnerability is even more pronounced when steering uses sparse autoencoder (SAE) features, which represent the standard for precise, interpretable model control.[12, 15] Injecting benign SAE feature directions increases the probability of harmful compliance by an additional 2% to 4% over random steering.[12, 15] The most effective jailbreaking SAE vectors correspond to ordinary, non-malicious concepts and fail to generalize across prompts, making systematic safety monitoring based on keywords or semantic content impossible.[12, 14]
Furthermore, combining 20 randomly sampled vectors that jailbreak a single prompt creates a universal attack vector.[12, 14, 16] This aggregated vector significantly increases harmful compliance on unseen prompts without requiring gradients, output logits, or training data.[12, 14, 16]
This vulnerability is exacerbated by Steering Externalities, where benign, task-focused steering (such as enforcing strict JSON formatting or helpful persona style transfer) erodes the model's safety alignment.[17] These benign interventions act as a force multiplier, boosting the success rate of standard black-box jailbreaks (such as CoP, PAIR, and TAP) to over 80%, and in some cases to nearly 99%, by reducing the model's internal safety margin.[17]
These inference-time exploits are mapped to traditional threat frameworks through tools like DwarfStar, which implement naive difference-in-means steering to democratize latent-space manipulation.[9] Under the MITRE ATLAS framework, this class of attack maps to AML.T0044 (Full ML Model Access), which enables AML.T0054 (LLM Jailbreak), AML.T0031 (Erode ML Model Integrity), and AML.T0015 (Evade ML Model).[9]
Type II: Privacy Exploitation and Training Data Extraction
A model’s internal representations also expose it to privacy leakage and training data extraction, allowing attackers to reconstruct sensitive training data, proprietary documents, or in-context resources.[13, 18, 19]
Pretraining induces highly structured and concentrated self-attention patterns for member samples that persist across layers and heads, creating a significant privacy vulnerability.[13, 20] Under the AttenMIA framework, attackers with white-box access exploit these transitional attention features to conduct Membership Inference Attacks (MIA).[13, 20] By performing active perturbations—such as token dropping or token replacement—attackers measure the resulting shifts in self-attention matrices.[13, 20] Because member samples exhibit substantially larger attention shifts under perturbation than non-members, attackers can identify training data with high precision, even at low false-positive rates, outperforming traditional MIAs based on output perplexity.[13, 20]
In Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines, private databases are vulnerable to continuous optimization attacks.[19, 21] The MARAGE framework formulates the extraction of retrieved contexts as a continuous optimization problem, optimizing an adversarial string appended to a user query.[19] By minimizing a loss function that aligns the generated tokens with the target RAG context, and using primacy weighting to focus attention on the starting portion of the context, MARAGE alters internal states to force the generation model to output the retrieved private data verbatim, bypassing system-level instruction-repetition blocks.[19]
Additionally, the Consistent Soft Prompt Targeted Data Extraction (CoSPED) framework trains consistent soft prompts to guide model behavior toward pretraining boundaries, enabling targeted suffix extraction given a prefix without altering the static model weights.[18]
Type III: Concept-Driven and Inductive Backdoors
Traditional backdoors require inserting discrete, human-detectable triggers (such as specific phrases or visual watermarks) into input samples.[22, 23] In contrast, representation-level backdoors operate directly on latent concepts.[22, 24]
The Representation Confusion (RepConfAttack) framework conceptualizes the decision process elicited by backdoor triggers as movement between representational spaces.[22] Rather than inserting external patches, RepConfAttack uses naturally occurring concepts (such as "water" identified via explainable AI tools) as the internal trigger.[22] By confusing these concepts in the representation space, the model's own internal conceptual tracking activates the backdoor during inference, making the attack highly stealthy and resistant to traditional sample-inspection defenses.[22, 23]
In contrast, Inductive Backdoors exploit narrow-to-broad generalization rather than direct memorization.[24] During fine-tuning on highly specific contexts, a model can infer hidden contextual cues (such as specific dates or formatting patterns).[24] When presented with a trigger that matches this inferred pattern (such as the temporal context "May 1984" in a Terminator-themed behavioral experiment, or specific 8-digit strings matching US President numbers), the model generalizes this representation to switch into a highly destructive, unaligned persona, despite never having seen the malicious behavior or the specific trigger during training.[24]
For multi-step, vision-language GUI agents, attacks like AgentRAE exploit native notification icons as visual triggers.[25] Through supervised contrastive learning, these subtle iconographic differences are separated and stabilized in the model's pooled representation space.[25] This establishes a persistent trigger-action mapping that redirects agent behaviors toward remote action execution while preserving normal performance on clean inputs.[25]

Perspective A: Classification of Representation-Level Defenses
To counter these vulnerabilities, defenders are shifting their focus from discrete prompt engineering and superficial output filtering to representation-level monitoring, adaptive inference steering, and post-training latent alignment.[28, 29, 34]
Type I: Latent Probing and Cognitive Monitoring
Latent probing and cognitive monitoring analyze early prompt representations within the model's residual stream to detect malicious intent before generation begins.[29, 30, 35]
Unlike early defenses that relied on linear probes, contemporary analysis demonstrates that features linked to jailbreak success are encoded non-linearly.[29, 30] By training Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) and Transformer classifiers on early prompt token representations, defenders can predict whether an incoming request will successfully jailbreak the model.[29, 30, 35] These probes achieve high accuracy in predicting jailbreak success using prompt activations alone, without requiring access to generated completions.[30, 35] However, a significant challenge is that different jailbreak methods exploit distinct, non-overlapping prompt features in the latent space, causing probes to fail to generalize to unseen attack categories.[29, 35]
For autonomous agents engaged in long-horizon tasks, defenders deploy frameworks like Agent-ToM.[36] This approach formulates monitoring as an inference problem, tracking the agent's beliefs, step-level intent hypotheses (with calibrated confidence), and expected actions relative to a task-aligned behavioral baseline.[36] This Reason-Verify-Refine pipeline discriminates between benign and covert malicious tool use, identifying safety violations before harmful actions are executed.[36]
Type II: Advanced Geometric and Adaptive Control
Inference-time defenses must modify activations safely without degrading general capability or causing repetitive outputs.[5, 7]
The ODESteer framework generalizes activation addition as a first-order approximation of a continuous trajectory governed by an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) [5, 26, 37]:
\frac{da(t)}{dt} = f(a(t))
In this framework, identifying the optimal steering direction is equivalent to designing a barrier function h(a) from control theory, which defines desirable and undesirable regions in the activation space.[5, 26, 38] ODESteer defines this barrier function using the non-linear log-density ratio between positive and negative activations:
h(a) = \log \frac{p_+(a)}{p_-(a)}
By numerically solving the ODE in multiple small, adaptive steps during decoding, the system dynamically guides activations away from harmful zones and toward safe, aligned states without degrading overall model coherence or benchmark performance.[5, 26] This adaptive approach yields significant alignment gains on benchmarks like TruthfulQA, UltraFeedback, and RealToxicityPrompts.[5, 26, 37]
Furthermore, aligned models often suffer from over-refusal, declining harmless requests that superficially resemble dangerous topics.[11, 39] Geometric analysis reveals that while intended harmful refusals are task-agnostic and lie along a single global vector, over-refusal directions are task-dependent.[11, 39] Over-refusal activations reside within benign task-representation clusters in mid-layer spaces (such as the peak task-identity layer at Layer 12) and span a higher-dimensional subspace.[11, 39] Consequently, simple global direction ablation (such as zeroing out the "refusal direction") is ineffective; defenders must apply task-conditioned geometric interventions to surgically correct over-refusals while preserving core safety boundaries.[11, 39] Toolkits like Obliteratus implement Expert-Granular Abliteration (EGA) to project out refusal subspaces on MoE routers, and CoT-Aware Ablation to orthogonalize refusal directions against reasoning-critical pathways.[40]
Through Trajectory Replay via Concept-Basis Reconstruction, defenders can extract refusal circuits from a well-aligned "donor" model and transfer them to a different "target" model (even across dense and MoE architectures).[41] Aligning layers through concept fingerprints and reconstructing refusal trajectories using concept atoms (vetted via SRA ridge regression to filter out protected features), this rank-one suppression update allows model-agnostic alignment transfer.[41]
Type III: Representational Alignment and Sequence-Aware Post-Training
To build deeper, more systemic defense mechanisms, researchers are developing post-training techniques designed to restructure how models represent instructions.[27, 28]
The Latent Instruction Representation Alignment (LIRA) framework addresses this by training the model to map malicious instruction representations directly onto nearby benign instruction spaces, forcing the model to process a harmful prompt as if it were safe.[27, 28] During LIRA training, backpropagation is modified to ignore gradients originating from response token positions.[27, 28] This focuses training updates strictly on the instruction bottleneck [27, 28]:
\nabla_{\theta} \mathcal{L} = \text{SAG}(\nabla_{\theta} \mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}})
By blocking the gradient flow from response tokens, SAG prevents the model from relying on superficial refusal text generation and ensures robust, structural alignment of the prompt representations.[27, 28] AdLIRA blocks over 99% of PEZ jailbreaks and effectively removes backdoor triggers.[27]
For unlearning and knowledge suppression, the ERASER framework identifies a Shared Forgetting Subspace by aggregating the principal directions of sensitive data domains.[32] During training, it applies projection-nullification to project hidden states away from this subspace [32]:
h_{\text{nullified}}^{(l)} = (I - P_{S_{\text{forget}}}) h^{(l)}
Supported by an auxiliary Disentangle-Head pairwise ranking loss, ERASER separates retained and forgotten domain representations, achieving clean unlearning that is highly robust to subsequent jailbreaks, membership inference, and retraining attacks.[32]

Operational Layer Calibration and Steering Dynamics
For practitioners developing or auditing latent-space interventions, understanding layer-specific representational dynamics is essential.[8] Middle layers generally represent the peak task-identity and conceptual regions, making them highly responsive to interventions, while late layers (the final 25%) encode style, register, and high-level behavioral properties like refusal or compliance.[8, 39]

Strategic Implications and Future Outlook
The tension between Perspective B and Perspective A highlights a fundamental shift in AI safety: the battleground has moved from discrete prompt space to continuous representation space.[9, 10, 28] This architectural evolution has deep strategic implications for model developers, red teams, and security practitioners.
First, the discovery of the "rogue scalpel" phenomenon challenges the long-held assumption that fine-grained interpretability automatically yields safer control.[14, 15] The fact that benign steering vectors (e.g., JSON formatting) and SAE-derived features can act as silent, non-linear safety bypasses highlights a structural vulnerability in Transformer architectures.[12, 17] When a model is steered to adopt a specific persona, style, or output structure, it undergoes a transformation in its latent space that often suppresses or collapses its refusal pathways.[12, 17] Consequently, organizations deploying locally hosted models with direct activation hooks (such as llama.cpp or custom inference engines) must treat model weight access and runtime activation hooks as critical security boundaries.[9]
Second, the structural separation between prompt-space and representation-space dynamics indicates that traditional black-box evaluation protocol is no longer sufficient.[9, 10] Because activation steering can push a model's state into areas of the high-dimensional latent space that are unreachable via standard discrete prompts, safety alignment must be assessed and defended at the representation layer.[10]
To address these challenges, the defense paradigm must transition toward structural interventions:
Move Beyond Superficial Fine-Tuning: Standard safety fine-tuning remains vulnerable to activation-level attacks because it only alters final-token behaviors.[27, 28] Techniques like LIRA, which utilize Sequence-Aware Gradients (SAG) to explicitly align instruction representations, should be integrated into default post-training pipelines to secure the latent bottleneck.[27, 28]
Implement Multi-Step, Adaptive Guardrails: One-step representation steering is highly brittle and easily bypassed or corrupted.[5, 7, 26] Implementing adaptive, control-theoretic frameworks like ODESteer allows for continuous, dynamic alignment checks during token generation, maintaining safety without compromising capability.[5, 26]
Establish Latent-Level Threat Monitoring: Traditional intrusion detection systems must be paired with real-time representation probing.[9, 29] Probes trained on prompt-side tokens can flag adversarial intent before generation begins, while agent monitoring tools like Agent-ToM can continuously audit autonomous agent trajectories for covert behavioral drift.[29, 30, 36]
Ultimately, representation engineering reveals that safety is not merely a property of discrete text, but a continuous geometric property of the model's internal states.[1, 2, 10] Securing future AI systems will require a rigorous, mathematically grounded defense of this latent geometry.[5, 27, 32]
