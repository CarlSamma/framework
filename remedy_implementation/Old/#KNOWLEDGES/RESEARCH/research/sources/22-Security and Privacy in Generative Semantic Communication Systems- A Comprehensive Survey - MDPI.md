Abstract
Semantic communication (SemCom) has emerged as a task-oriented communication paradigm that prioritizes meaning delivery over exact bit recovery. The integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) into SemCom further enables knowledge-guided inference, multimodal reconstruction, and semantic compression through architectures such as large language models, variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, and diffusion models. At the same time, this integration introduces new security and privacy risks, including semantic eavesdropping, model inversion, semantic jamming, covert backdoors, prompt manipulation, and knowledge-base leakage, which are not adequately captured by conventional communication security models. In this survey, we provide a security-centric review of GenAI-assisted semantic communication systems by organizing the literature according to threat models, attack surfaces, defence strategies, and semantic modalities across text, image, and multimodal settings. The survey was conducted using IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, SpringerLink, arXiv, and Google Scholar. Approximately 180 papers were initially screened, and 53 representative studies published between 2021 and 2026 were selected for detailed review. Based on this analysis, we classify the major threats into adversarial perturbation, jamming, poisoning and backdoor attacks, privacy leakage and semantic eavesdropping, and generative-model-specific vulnerabilities involving diffusion, large language models, and multimodal foundation models. We further map the corresponding defences, including adversarial training, model ensembling, semantic-aware encryption, diffusion-guided denoising, privacy-preserving representation learning, and secure resource allocation. The survey also identifies persistent open challenges, including the lack of standardized semantic security metrics, unified benchmarks, cross-layer evaluation frameworks, and robust defences for GenAI-native and multimodal semantic communication systems. Overall, this work provides a structured reference for the design of secure, trustworthy, and attack-resilient generative semantic communication systems for future intelligent networks.

1. Introduction
The emergence of semantic communication (SemCom) represents a fundamental shift from conventional bit-level transmission toward the exchange of task-relevant meaning. Rather than guaranteeing exact symbol recovery, SemCom seeks to convey only the information necessary for successful task execution, thereby improving communication efficiency under bandwidth, latency, and energy constraints. By leveraging deep learning and contextual awareness, semantic communication systems preserve Semantic integrity even when traditional signal-level metrics degrade [ 1, 2]. This paradigm is particularly well suited for intelligent applications such as autonomous driving, immersive environments, and the Metaverse, where decision accuracy matters more than bit-perfect reconstruction.
Recent advances have further accelerated this paradigm through the integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) [ 3, 4, 5] into semantic communication pipelines. Generative models including large language models (LLMs), VAEs, and diffusion networks enable receivers to reconstruct semantically meaningful content from compact semantic representations rather than explicit symbol streams. Such capabilities support knowledge-grounded generation, multimodal semantic transmission—where information from multiple modalities such as text, vision, and audio is jointly encoded and semantically fused, and content-aware compression, allowing GenAI-assisted SemCom systems to outperform conventional encoders in both semantic fidelity and spectral efficiency [ 6, 7]. As a result, generative semantic communication has emerged as a core architectural component for future intelligent and task-oriented networks.
At the core of this paradigm is semantic data, which refers to meaning-bearing information such as intent, context, and task relevance, usually represented as latent embeddings, semantic tokens, or knowledge-assisted features. Although these representations facilitate efficient compression and effective job execution, they may also reveal important structural and contextual information. Consequently, safeguarding semantic data necessitates security measures that extend beyond traditional encryption or error correction, as semantic meaning may still be deducible despite the maintenance of physical-layer confidentiality.
The introduction of GenAI into semantic communication substantially reshapes the associated security and privacy landscape. Traditional communication security focuses on protecting symbols and signals; however, semantic communication systems are vulnerable to attacks that directly target meaning and model behaviour. Adversarial perturbations can induce semantic misinterpretation at the receiver without significantly affecting metrics such as BER, exposing a fundamental mismatch between conventional security guarantees and semantic correctness [ 3]. Multi-domain adversarial attacks further amplify this threat by jointly manipulating source data and channel-level signals, resulting in semantic distortion across both physical and semantic layers. Similar attack–defence dynamics have been observed in task-oriented regression systems, where gradient-based adversarial perturbations substantially degrade prediction accuracy, and runtime defences such as adversarial training and distillation recover performance at the cost of additional complexity [ 8].
These risks are intensified when generative models are employed for semantic reconstruction. Generative encoders and decoders, diffusion-based reconstruction modules, and knowledge-assisted semantic representations may unintentionally retain sensitive information, making them susceptible to semantic eavesdropping and model inversion attacks—even under secure channel conditions. Recent studies demonstrate that adversaries can exploit generative priors to reconstruct private semantic content with high accuracy, highlighting that semantic confidentiality cannot be ensured through channel-level protection alone [ 9, 10, 11]. Moreover, GenAI-based SemCom pipelines are exposed to poisoning, backdoor, and inference attacks, particularly in distributed or federated environments where semantic models and knowledge bases are collaboratively trained and shared.
Beyond privacy leakage, generative semantic communication systems are increasingly threatened by semantic manipulation attacks. Unlike classical adversarial attacks that target classification outputs, semantic-level attacks aim to alter reconstructed meaning while preserving perceptual or signal-level quality. Covert semantic backdoors and poisoning-based attacks demonstrate that malicious behaviours can be embedded directly into semantic feature spaces and triggered only under specific contextual or channel conditions [ 12, 13, 14]. In safety-critical scenarios such as autonomous driving and intelligent connected vehicles, such attacks may lead to catastrophic decisions without triggering conventional anomaly detectors. Generative decoders further exacerbate this risk by amplifying subtle semantic manipulations into coherent yet misleading outputs [ 4, 15].
The growing adoption of LLMs and multimodal foundation models as semantic encoders and reasoning agents introduces additional vulnerabilities. In this context, a semantic encoder maps raw inputs into compact, task-relevant latent representations that capture meaning (e.g., intent, objects, relations) rather than exact symbol-level information. LLM-based semantic communication systems have been shown to suffer from prompt manipulation, output-representation backdoors, and transfer-based black-box attacks that persist across downstream tasks [ 16, 17, 18]. Similarly, multimodal generative systems inherit vulnerabilities from vision–language models, where semantic perturbations and backdoor triggers can simultaneously compromise perception and reasoning [ 19, 20]. These attacks operate at semantic and reasoning levels rather than the signal layer, rendering traditional threat models insufficient.
From a defensive perspective, recent research has proposed a variety of countermeasures, including semantic-aware encryption, adaptive noise injection, diffusion-guided denoising, dynamic adversarial filtering, and privacy-preserving representation learning [ 21, 22, 23]. While these approaches demonstrate promising security–performance trade-offs, they are often evaluated under limited threat assumptions, single-modality settings, or idealized attacker models. Surveys consistently highlight the lack of standardized semantic security metrics, unified benchmarks, and cross-layer evaluation methodologies tailored to GenAI-enabled semantic communication [ 24, 25, 26, 27].
Motivated by these challenges, this survey provides a systematic and security-centric review of Generative AI-assisted semantic communication systems. We focus on how generative models reshape semantic data representations, expand attack surfaces, and necessitate new defence paradigms beyond traditional communication security. By organizing the literature according to semantic attack models, defence strategies, and generative optimization mechanisms, this work aims to bridge the gap between semantic robustness and security assurance, offering a foundational reference for the design of reliable, task-oriented, and secure GenAI-enabled communication systems in future networks.

Although several recent surveys have reviewed semantic communication from perspectives such as architecture, resource management, privacy, and general security [ 24, 25], a clear gap remains in the security-focused analysis of Generative AI-assisted semantic communication systems. In particular, existing survey works do not systematically examine how Generative AI reshapes the security landscape by introducing new attack surfaces at the generative, semantic, and reasoning levels, including prompt manipulation, knowledge-base leakage, diffusion-space perturbations, latent backdoors, and multimodal model vulnerabilities. This survey addresses that gap by providing a security-centric review of GenAI-assisted semantic communication, organized through a unified taxonomy of threat models, attack surfaces, defence strategies, and semantic modalities. In addition, the paper maps major attack categories to corresponding defences and highlights open research challenges related to semantic security metrics, unified benchmarks, cross-layer evaluation, and robust protection for GenAI-native and multimodal systems.
This survey reviews literature published between 2021 and 2026 on the security and privacy of semantic communication, with particular emphasis on Generative AI-assisted semantic communication systems. The literature search was conducted using IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, SpringerLink, arXiv, and Google Scholar with keywords including “semantic Communication security”, “Generative semantic Communication”, “semantic attacks”, “semantic defences”, “semantic privacy”, and related combinations. In total, approximately 180 records were initially identified. After removing duplicate and clearly irrelevant records, the remaining studies were screened based on title, abstract, and full-text relevance. The inclusion criteria covered studies that addressed security, privacy, attack models, defence mechanisms, or trustworthy design in semantic communication, generative semantic communication, or closely related GenAI-enabled semantic systems. The exclusion criteria removed duplicate records, non-English papers, studies without substantial technical relevance to semantic communication security, works focused only on conventional communication security without semantic aspects, and papers lacking sufficient methodological or experimental detail. After this filtering process, 53 representative studies were selected for detailed review. These studies were considered representative because they collectively cover major threat models, defence strategies, semantic modalities, and architectural settings discussed in current GenAI-assisted semantic communication research.

2. Background and Related Works
This section provides a structured background for GenAI-SemCom from a security-oriented perspective. Instead of presenting isolated definitions, we review foundational concepts alongside representative state-of-the-art studies, emphasizing how generative models reshape semantic representations, attack surfaces, and defence mechanisms. The discussion is organized into five subsections covering Generative AI, semantic Data, semantic communication, semantic Attacks, and semantic defences.
2.1. Generative AI
Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) refers to a class of learning-based models capable of synthesizing structured content—such as text, images, audio, or multimodal data—by learning underlying data distributions. Representative architectures include LLMs, VAEs, GANs, and diffusion models. In the context of semantic communication, GenAI enables receivers to reconstruct semantically meaningful content from compact or incomplete semantic representations, significantly reducing transmission overhead.
Recent works demonstrate that generative models play a central role in next-generation semantic communication systems by replacing deterministic decoders with probabilistic semantic reconstruction engines. Knowledge-base-enabled semantic communication frameworks leverage GenAI to reconstruct content based on shared intent, task knowledge, and contextual priors rather than raw symbols [ 28]. Similarly, diffusion-based semantic communication systems utilize iterative denoising to recover high-fidelity semantics from highly compressed Semanticmaps, outperforming traditional reconstruction-oriented schemes under severe channel degradation [ 29].
However, the inclusion of generative models fundamentally alters the security landscape. Generative priors may unintentionally amplify semantic leakage, hallucinate misleading content, or propagate poisoned representations learned during training. Surveys consistently report that GenAI-driven semantic communication systems expand the attack surface beyond the physical and semantic layers to include generative and reasoning components [ 4]. As a result, security analysis must account for both generative robustness and semantic correctness rather than channel reliability alone.
A comparative view shows that different generative architectures contribute distinct advantages to semantic communication. Knowledge-base-driven and LLM-oriented approaches are particularly effective for context-aware reasoning and semantic reconstruction from sparse inputs, but they depend heavily on prior alignment and shared knowledge consistency, as shown in knowledge-base-enabled semantic communication frameworks [ 28]. In contrast, diffusion-based models provide strong reconstruction quality and robustness under severe degradation, yet they usually introduce higher computational cost and larger inference latency, as demonstrated by diffusion-based generative semantic communication systems [ 29]. GAN- and VAE-based models offer efficient generation and compression benefits, but they are often more sensitive to instability, hallucination, or distributional mismatch in practical deployment [ 6]. Therefore, the choice of generative architecture in GenAI-SemCom involves a trade-off among semantic fidelity, computational efficiency, contextual reasoning, and security robustness.
In summary, GenAI is not merely a reconstruction tool in semantic communication; it reshapes both system capability and system risk. As discussed by Ren et al. [ 4], its benefits in semantic recovery and compression must therefore be evaluated together with its expanded exposure to leakage, hallucination, poisoning, and reasoning-level attacks.
2.2. Semantic Data
Semantic data refers to task-relevant, meaning-bearing representations extracted from raw source information. Unlike conventional data symbols, semantic data encapsulates intent, contextual relevance, and knowledge relationships required for downstream decision-making. In GenAI-SemCom systems, semantic data is typically represented in latent embedding spaces, structured knowledge graphs, or compressed semantic tokens aligned with task objectives.
Knowledge-assisted semantic communication frameworks highlight that semantic data is inherently context-dependent and often intertwined with private user information [ 30]. Shared or personalized knowledge bases enable efficient semantic reconstruction but also introduce privacy risks when adversaries exploit semantic correlations or auxiliary knowledge to infer sensitive attributes. Studies show that semantic representations may leak significantly more information than expected, even when signal-level metrics suggest secure transmission [ 26].
Generative semantic communication complicates semantic data by allowing receivers to reconstruct high-dimensional information from minimum semantic cues. This improves efficiency but allows attackers to employ generative priors for semantic inference and inversion attacks. Recent investigations have shown that semantic data safety requires more than encryption or noise injection. Generative thinking can nevertheless recover semantic meaning, as found in the study [ 25]. These findings demand semantically aware, GenAI-specific privacy and security techniques.
From a comparative perspective, semantic data representations differ in both utility and exposure. As discussed by Liuet al. [ 30], latent embeddings are compact and efficient for transmission, but their compressed structure may still preserve sensitive semantic attributes that support inference and privacy leakage. Knowledge-graph and knowledge-assisted representations improve contextual interpretability and reconstruction quality, yet they also introduce additional privacy risks because attackers may exploit semantic relations and auxiliary knowledge [ 26]. Semantic tokens provide flexible task-oriented abstraction, but their security depends strongly on how much high-level meaning is retained and how easily such meaning can be reconstructed by generative models. Thus, richer semantic representations generally improve efficiency and task performance, but they also increase the risk of semantic leakage [ 25].
In summary, semantic data should be viewed as both an enabler of efficient communication and a direct security object that requires protection. Its design must balance compactness, usefulness, and interpretability against privacy leakage and inference vulnerability.
2.3. Semantic Communication
In contrast to Shannon-style source–channel separation, semantic communication is characterized by the simultaneous optimization of semantic extraction, transmission, and task execution. A semantic encoder is a component of a typical semantic communication system. It is responsible for extracting features that are relevant to the job at hand, while a channel encoder is responsible for mapping semantic representations to wireless signals. Finally, a semantic decoder is responsible for reconstructing meaning at the receiver. Generative models that are capable of probabilistic semantic reconstruction are frequently used in GenAI-SemCom to either replace or supplement the decoder when it is used.
Task-oriented semantic communication prioritizes decision accuracy or task success over signal fidelity, enabling robust performance under extreme compression or noisy channels. Experimental studies demonstrate that semantic communication systems can maintain high task accuracy even when traditional metrics such as BER or PSNR degrade significantly [ 3]. Generative semantic communication further enhances robustness by allowing receivers to infer missing semantics using learned priors rather than explicit retransmission.
Despite these advantages, semantic communication systems are inherently vulnerable to semantic distortion and manipulation. Because correctness is defined at the Semanti core task level, attacks that preserve signal quality while altering meaning may go undetected. Surveys on resource management and semantic radio access networks highlight that semantic communication architectures significantly increase the attack surface by introducing shared models, task-awareness, and adaptive reconstruction [ 24, 27]. Consequently, semantic communication requires security guarantees that extend beyond physical-layer confidentiality.
Comparatively, conventional communication systems are optimized for faithful symbol recovery, whereas semantic communication systems are optimized for task success and meaning preservation. As established by Xie et al. [ 1] and Luo et al. [ 2], this gives semantic communication a clear advantage under bandwidth and noise constraints, since useful decisions may still be achieved even when signal-level fidelity degrades. However, Sagduyu et al. [ 3] show that the same shift also creates a major limitation: performance can appear satisfactory under traditional metrics while semantic meaning has already been manipulated. GenAI-assisted semantic communication further strengthens robustness through prior-driven reconstruction, but this benefit is coupled with greater dependence on shared models, learned priors, and adaptive decoding, all of which enlarge the semantic attack surface [ 6].
In summary, semantic communication provides higher task efficiency and robustness than bit-centric designs, but it also changes what must be protected. As a result, secure semantic communication requires evaluation criteria that jointly consider transmission quality, semantic correctness, and resilience to meaning-level attacks.
2.4. Semantic Attack
The purpose of semantic attacks is not to disrupt signal transmission but rather to compromise the meaning, intent, or task outcome of semantic communication.These attacks exploit learned semantic representations, shared knowledge bases, or generative reconstruction methods, often without causing noticeable degradation in conventional signal-level metrics.
A key concern in semantic attacks is that semantic integrity may be compromised even when traditional security indicators suggest normal operation. This mismatch makes meaning-centered threat models and evaluation criteria essential for semantic communication systems.
Poisoning and backdoor attacks represent a particularly severe threat in GenAI-SemCom systems. Covert semantic backdoor attacks embed triggers directly into semantic feature spaces, enabling selective semantic deletion or manipulation during inference while maintaining high perceptual quality [ 12]. Channel-triggered backdoors further demonstrate that semantic attacks can be activated implicitly through wireless channel conditions rather than explicit input patterns [ 14]. Recent studies also reveal that generative reconstruction pipelines are vulnerable to latent manipulation attacks, where adversarial biases accumulate across diffusion steps and result in controlled semantic drift [ 15].
A comparative examination shows that semantic attacks differ not only in mechanism but also in stealth and activation conditions. Xu et al. [ 12] show that poisoning and backdoor attacks are especially dangerous because they can remain dormant during normal operation and activate only under specific triggers, making them difficult to detect. Wan et al. [ 14] further increase this stealth by embedding activation conditions in transmission environments rather than visible input patterns. In addition, Xi and Zhu [ 15] show that latent manipulation in diffusion-based pipelines can gradually alter semantic meaning across generative steps without obvious signal-level degradation. Overall, these methods are stronger in semantic communication than conventional jamming or noise-based disruption because they directly target task meaning rather than transmission quality alone.
In summary, semantic attacks exploit the gap between signal fidelity and semantic correctness. Their main danger lies in their ability to remain covert while inducing meaningful task failure, which makes semantic-level threat modeling and evaluation a central requirement for secure GenAI-SemCom design.
2.5. Semantic Defence
Semantic defence mechanisms are designed to preserve semantic integrity, maintain privacy, and ensure task reliability amidst adversarial perturbations. On the other hand, semantic defences work at the representation, generative, and task-execution levels, in contrast to traditional defences, which concentrate on signal protection.
Learning-based defences, such as adversarial training, enhance robustness by subjecting semantic encoders to adversarial perturbations while they are being trained [ 31]. Model ensembling approaches, such as ROME, utilize variety across models that have been trained independently in order to reduce black-box semantic jamming without necessitating adversary knowledge [ 32]. Policies that are based on reinforcement learning are able to alter transmission and encoding procedures in response to jamming or interference in dynamic environments [ 33].
There has recently been an increase in the pursuit of generative-aware defences. Denoising and semantic correction methods utilizing diffusion leverage the generative capabilities of receivers to restore clean semantics from contaminated representations [ 10]. In vehicular communication contexts, semantic-aware encryption selectively retains significant semantic characteristics, hence attaining favorable security-efficiency trade-offs [ 34]. Dynamic adversarial defence frameworks demonstrate that semantic noise filtering and contrastive alignment can significantly mitigate the effects of attacks without necessitating the retraining of the foundational models [ 21].
Furthermore, studies repeatedly demonstrate that existing defences are fragmented, modality-specific, and evaluated under constrained threat assumptions [ 24, 25]. In parallel, recent work has shown that network-level design choices, such as topology optimization, can intrinsically enhance robustness against neural attacks, offering a complementary defence dimension beyond data augmentation or adversarial training [ 35]. This is despite the fact that the results of these surveys are encouraging. Furthermore, increases in security frequently result in non-negligible costs in terms of latency, energy consumption, or job accuracy, which reveals an intrinsic trade-off between security and performance. Because of these restrictions, there is an increased demand for defensive frameworks that are unified, cognizant of generative processes, and performance-coupled in order to ensure secure semantic communication.
On a comparative basis, semantic defences that are currently in use address distinct threat classes, which in turn results in varying advantages and limitations. Adversarial training improves resilience to known perturbation patterns, as shown by Peng et al. [ 36], but it typically depends on the attack assumptions seen during training and may not generalize well to adaptive or novel threats. Model ensembling enhances robustness through diversity and reduces reliance on a single semantic pathway, but Zhou et al. [ 32] show that this comes at the cost of increased storage and coordination overhead. Reinforcement-learning-based strategies are effective in dynamic environments such as jamming scenarios, yet Liu et al. [ 33] indicate that they require careful optimization and may be difficult to stabilize. Generative-aware defences, such as diffusion-guided denoising and semantic correction, show promise for recovering distorted semantics, although He et al. [ 10] demonstrate that they often introduce additional complexity and inference cost. Semantic-aware encryption provides efficient privacy protection in vehicular semantic communication, but Wang et al. [ 34] show that it does not by itself address broader robustness and model-level attack surfaces.
In summary, current semantic defences are useful but fragmented. A major research need is the development of unified defence frameworks that can jointly address robustness, privacy, efficiency, and cross-modal security in GenAI-assisted semantic communication systems.

This subsection addresses threats that directly alter semantic representations or task results, frequently without substantially diminishing signal-level metrics. These assaults illustrate the intrinsic discord between bit-level dependability and Semanticcoherence.

The attack exploits shared semantic encoders such as BERT, which map input sentences into latent semantic embeddings. The attack targets the encoder's latent representation space and aims to maximize semantic drift at the receiver. Experiments on the SNLI dataset using a BERT-based encoder–decoder architecture evaluate semantic distortion via BLEU score and cosine similarity under additive Gaussian noise. Results show a significant degradation in semantic accuracy, with BLEU decreasing from 0.72 to 0.46, while adversarial training partially restores performance to 0.59. The study demonstrates the vulnerability of shared semantic encoders in SemCom systems, although the white-box assumption limits the generalizability of the attack.
The current investigation explores the security aspects of semantic communication systems, specifically examining the inherent security of these systems when implemented with deep neural networks. This study conceptualizes semantic communication through an end-to-end autoencoder paired with a semantic task classifier, highlighting the dependence on deep neural networks as a critical security weakness. The primary focus is the structured development of multi-domain adversarial attacks, in which adversaries alter semantic meaning either within the input data domain (such as computer vision attacks on the encoder input) or within the wireless signal domain (including over-the-air perturbations aimed at the decoder), along with their synchronized integration. The study employs adversarial machine learning methods like FGSM, BIM, PGD, and C&W to showcase both targeted and non-targeted attacks, resulting in significant semantic misclassifications with minimal perturbation power. Findings from experiments conducted on the MNIST dataset indicate a significant decline in semantic task accuracy, plummeting to levels as low as 0.1, despite maintaining a low reconstruction loss ( MSE ≈ 0.09 ). This highlights the potential for semantic integrity to be undermined, even when the quality at the signal level seems satisfactory. The findings indicate that attacks spanning multiple domains are more effective than those confined to a single domain, attaining elevated success rates with minimal perturbation-to-noise ratios, and greatly surpassing the effects of traditional Gaussian jamming. The discussion [ 3] on interactive training as a means to enhance performance is present, yet the evaluation of defensive strategies is somewhat limited, and there is a lack of concrete countermeasures implemented. The authors assert that semantic communication systems exhibit significant susceptibility to adversarial manipulation in various domains, highlighting an urgent requirement for defence strategies. Nonetheless, the absence of practical defences, dependence on simplistic datasets, and emphasis on white-box threat models restrict the immediate applicability of the results.
This publication introduces a security-centric semantic communication framework for textual transmission, explicitly examining the influence of semantic noise, which is recognized as a significant yet insufficiently examined hazard separate from physical channel noise. Ref. [ 36] primary contribution to security is the systematic classification of semantic noise into two distinct types: literal semantic noise, which includes grammatical errors and word substitutions, and adversarial semantic noise, characterized by the introduction of malicious perturbations into the embedding space that lead to semantic misinterpretation. The proposed R-DeepSC system builds upon the DeepSC framework by incorporating a calibrated self-attention mechanism, which is directed by an error-detection network to mitigate erroneous tokens. Additionally, it employs adversarial training based on FGM to improve resilience against embedding-level attacks. The analysis of performance on the Europarl dataset across AWGN and Rayleigh fading channels reveals that R-DeepSC consistently surpasses traditional communication systems and baseline DeepSC, especially regarding BLEU and BERTScore metrics, particularly in scenarios with high semantic noise ratios (up to 60%) and low SNR conditions. The findings indicate that semantic fidelity diminishes at a significantly slower rate than the baseline comparisons, thereby confirming the efficacy of the suggested defences. Nonetheless, the research presumes white-box access for adversarial training, concentrates solely on text modalities, and overlooks more potent or adaptive threats like backdoors or multi-domain semantic attacks. This work serves as an essential reference for security in semantic communication, yet it primarily addresses robustness against semantic noise, lacking a broader examination of various semantic security threats.
The present paper [ 37] offers a thorough security-focused examination of semantic communication systems, carefully investigating the emergence of security and privacy concerns at the semantic encoder, knowledge base, physical channel, and decoder. The authors delineate four principal categories of attacks—adversarial attacks, poisoning/backdoor attacks, network attacks, and inference attacks—and explicitly clarify how these threats diverge from traditional bit-level communication attacks by focusing on semantic meaning and contextual knowledge. The study delineates a three-tier semantic communication paradigm (technical, semantic, and effectiveness layers) and correlates specific assaults and compromised security attributes to each tier. This analysis examines adversarial training, denoising, model optimization, data preprocessing, differential privacy, homomorphic encryption, and secure network architecture from a defence standpoint, highlighting their relevance to semantic pipelines. A significant contribution is a case study on federated semantic encoder/decoder training utilizing zero-knowledge proofs, which experimentally diminishes membership inference attack accuracy to approximately 50% (close to random guessing), at the expense of substantial computation (approximately 110 s for proof generation) and communication overhead (approximately 50–650 MB). The paper presents robust conceptual coverage and quantitative overhead analysis; however, it is deficient in task-level semantic performance metrics (e.g., SSIM, BLEU, task success rate) under attack, offers minimal discussion specific to GenAI, and fails to assess defences against contemporary generative threats such as prompt manipulation or diffusion-based attacks. Overall, it functions as a fundamental security assessment for semantic communication, although it distinctly lacks provisions for GenAI-driven, attack-aware, and performance-coupled security evaluations, which your study explicitly addresses.
Ref. [ 38] analyses adversarial attacks and their defences in semantic communication systems for vehicular metaverse applications, emphasizing the susceptibility of semantic-level perception and communication in safety-critical contexts. The authors examine semantic adversarial perturbation assaults, wherein gradient-based perturbations are introduced into semantic features or perceptual outputs to provoke semantic misinterpretation at the receiver, notwithstanding the stability of standard signal-level metrics. These assaults employ iterative gradient-based techniques aimed at deep semantic encoders utilized in vision-oriented vehicular communication systems. The system is architecturally designed as an end-to-end vehicular semantic communication framework, incorporating perception modules, semantic encoders, wireless transmission, and task-oriented decoders, which mirror practical V2X and metaverse deployment scenarios. To address these concerns, the research provides semantic-aware defence mechanisms, including adversarial training, semantic consistency regularization, and robust feature alignment, which seek to stabilize semantic representations against adversarial perturbations. Experimental findings indicate that after an attack, semantic task accuracy declines by over 35–50%, but the suggested defences restore performance to within 5–10% of the clean accuracy. The defences diminish attack success rates by roughly 40–60% across various SNR levels, with relatively slight deterioration ( ≈ 3 – 8 % ) in clean performance. The results indicate a distinct trade-off between security and performance, affirming that semantic resilience can be significantly enhanced with acceptable overhead. The study, although not utilizing extensive generative models like LLMs or diffusion networks, offers a quantitatively proven attack-defence analysis at the semantic layer, rendering it a pertinent security reference for semantic communication systems in automotive and immersive contexts.
The robustness against black-box assaults, in which the attacker lacks access to model parameters or gradients, is the main emphasis in [ 18] on the study of a secure semantic communication model for metaverse scenarios. The authors examine black-box semantic attacks that utilize query access to the semantic communication system to deduce decision limits and create perturbations that result in semantic misinterpretation and task failure at the receiver. These attacks are executed by systematically probing the system and refining perturbations based on output responses instead of internal gradients, rendering them feasible for real-world metaverse applications. The suggested system is architecturally designed as an end-to-end semantic communication framework, comprising a deep semantic encoder, a wireless transmission module, and a task-oriented semantic decoder, specifically developed for immersive metaverse applications. To counter black-box threats, the research presents a secure semantic communication model that integrates resilient semantic feature extraction, noise-aware semantic reconstruction, and defensive regularization to diminish susceptibility to adversarial queries and perturbations. Performance assessments indicate that baseline semantic communication systems experience task accuracy declines of about 30–45% under black-box attacks, while the proposed defence restores performance to within 5–10% of the clean accuracy level. The defence diminishes attack success rates by roughly 40–55% under various channel circumstances, incurring just a slight cost of approximately 5–8% regarding latency or reconstruction quality. The findings indicate that integrating security-conscious design at the semantic layer can markedly enhance resilience against realistic black-box attacks; however, the research does not address threats specific to Generative AI, such as LLM-based prompt manipulation or diffusion-driven semantic attacks, thereby presenting these as avenues for future investigation in GenAI-enabled semantic communication security research.

3.2. Semantic Jamming and Anti-Jamming Strategies
This subsection groups works where the attacker injects interference aimed at degrading semantic fidelity, and defences rely on learning-based adaptation or semantic-layer robustness rather than physical-layer suppression.
The authors in [ 33] create an intelligent anti-jamming framework for UAV-assisted SemCom on mobile edge computing (MEC) networks, where message integrity and task offloading are seriously threatened by hostile interference. 3.3. Backdoor and Poisoning Attacks on Semantic Models
This subsection covers training-time and inference-time poisoning attacks that implant covert behaviours into semantic communication systems, including generative and perception-driven pipelines.
The authors introduce CSBA (Covert Semantic Backdoor Attack) in [ 12], a novel attack framework that targets SemCom systems in ICVs. The research only examines the offensive aspect, seeking to illustrate that semantic-level feature poisoning can discreetly alter machine perception without activating conventional defence systems. In contrast to conventional backdoors that depend on observable input alterations, CSBA integrates triggers directly into the semantic feature space. The purpose is to eliminate a specific Semantic feature (e.g., a road sign or pedestrian) from reconstructed images while preserving overall visual quality, hence maintaining a high PSNR and remaining undetected. The architecture comprises three essential stages: Target Semantic Localization (TSL) employs DeepLabV3 to identify and delineate target semantics; Target Semantic Seamless Integration (TSSI) utilizes PLaMa (Patch-wise Latent Masking) for context-aware inpainting of the excised region; and Semantic Backdoor Model Poisoning (SBMP) introduces backdoor behaviour through adversarial fine-tuning on ostensibly pristine data. PLaMa facilitates seamless incorporation of masked regions into latent space, guaranteeing that semantic alterations do not produce discernible pixel-level artefacts. The assault is initiated solely under particular Semanticconditions, rendering it both selective and covert. The system is assessed on the Cityscapes dataset utilizing measures including PSNR, LPSNR, and ASR. Results indicate that ASR surpasses 80% in high SNR situations, with minimal PSNR loss, demonstrating the attack's efficacy and inconspicuousness. The article emphasizes the necessity for more sophisticated semantic-level detection methods, as conventional defences are inadequate in identifying CSBA due to its hidden activation and execution within the feature space. This work introduces a novel and stealthy backdoor paradigm in SemCom, operating entirely in the semantic feature space. However, its dependence on pre-poisoned models and ideal attack conditions limits its applicability in well-controlled or real-time learning environments.

A serious semantic-level security flaw that transcends classification tasks and reaches pixel-level semantic reasoning—the basis of semantic communication and perception systems—is exposed by this study's [ 40] investigation of hidden backdoor attacks against semantic segmentation models. The principal methodological addition is a nuanced backdoor assault paradigm, wherein just the pixels of designated objects are malevolently relabeled at the object level, rather than the entire image, facilitating more specific and less detectable attacks. Ref. [ 14] delves into a channel-triggered backdoor attack (CT-BA) that targets wireless semantic picture reconstruction, illuminating a serious and yet unexplored security risk in SemCom systems. 3.4. Privacy Leakage, Semantic Eavesdropping, and Model Inversion

To address privacy vulnerabilities in SemCom systems, the authors of [ 30] suggest a layered design focused on developing a defence mechanism against semantic-level eavesdropping. The primary threat involves passive attackers leveraging access to either personal or global knowledge to infer sensitive meanings from transmitted content. The proposed architecture comprises a knowledge management layer that generates personalized knowledge graphs based on user context, and a data transmission layer that encodes input data through entity recognition, graph embedding, and mixed-token alignment. The mixed-token alignment approach integrates and aligns tokens from both private and global knowledge graphs into a unified semantic space to improve interpretability while concealing sensitive context. The attacker is modeled as a non-intrusive eavesdropper capable of observing encoded messages and possessing partial or full access to the global KB, enabling correlation-based inference. At the receiver, semantic reconstruction is executed utilizing aligned graph semantics to guarantee contextual accuracy while safeguarding private information. The authors assess their system through a detailed structural analysis of semantic leakage paths, comparing information exposure across different threat models—including global-only, personal-only, and hybrid knowledge scenarios. The objective is to facilitate context-aware SemCom while safeguarding sensitive information from inference assaults. The research presents a modular and extendable framework for knowledge-driven privacy in SemCom; nonetheless, its absence of empirical results from real-world datasets indicates a necessity for future validation in dynamic contexts.
The Cross-Layer Semantic Security Rate (CLSSR) is a newly proposed metric in [ 41] that quantifies security in SemCom systems by integrating both physical-layer confidentiality and semantic task reliability. CLSSR is designed to measure the effective secure transmission rate, considering how well a system preserves signal secrecy and semantic correctness simultaneously. The semantic data used in this study is text-based, and the system performs goal-directed tasks such as sentence classification, where successful task completion is the performance indicator. The primary concept is to close the disconnect between physical-layer protection and semantic-level robustness through dynamic cross-layer resource optimization. The system is formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where the transmitter adaptively modifies power allocation, beamforming vectors, and semantic encoding parameters to maximize CLSSR. The optimization task is solved as a Mixed Integer Nonlinear Programming (MINLP) problem, which jointly handles continuous and discrete variables under nonlinear constraints. A policy iteration algorithm, rooted in reinforcement learning, is used to refine action policies over time based on expected CLSSR improvement. Evaluation is conducted under Rayleigh fading channels with MIMO/MISO setups and demonstrates that CLSSR can improve by up to 295% compared to physical-layer-only approaches, achieving 100% task reliability under moderate noise. The adversary is modeled as an eavesdropper attempting to disrupt either semantic task outcomes or intercept meaningful content via channel inference. The attack is implicit, representing degradation of either semantic reliability or information leakage under unprotected transmission. This framework introduces a cross-layer optimization-driven approach to semantic security; however, its applicability to real-world settings may be constrained by idealized assumptions and limited semantic task diversity.

Using multi-modal prompts for secure and accurate picture reconstruction, the authors of [ 43] suggest a new system called Generative AI-aided semantic communication (SemCom) that eliminates the requirement for collaborative training of the encoder and decoder. This study is centered on defence and tackles two primary challenges: significant computing demands arising from cooperative encoder-decoder training and security risks during prompt transmission. The proposed framework incorporates textual prompts extracted using the Multi-modal Mixture of Encoder-Decoder (MED), which applies contrastive and language modeling objectives. In addition, it employs visual prompts generated through Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM), which capture image structure from Gaussian noise in latent space. The adversary, referred to as the warden, is modeled as a passive detector that uses binary hypothesis testing to decide whether or not a transmission is occurring. To optimize system performance, the framework jointly adjusts the transmit power (referred to in the paper as “gearbox power”), the jamming power, and the number of diffusion steps, which represent how many iterations the generative model uses to reconstruct the target message. These parameters are efficiently tuned using a resource allocator built upon a GDM, which guides secure and low-profile prompt transmission under energy constraints. The threat model presumes a passive eavesdropper (warden) seeking to identify transmission activity, rather than interfering with semantic content. The evaluation results indicate that the suggested GDM-based scheme attains a higher SSIM, a perceptual metric ranging from 0 to 1 that measures the similarity between original and reconstructed pictures, achieving values as high as 0.9. It also reduces the bit error probability (BEP) under energy restrictions. In contrast to DRL-based baselines that utilize deep reinforcement learning for optimizing communication parameters through incentive feedback, the GDM-based approach provides expedited convergence and enhanced semantic correctness. The framework adeptly circumvents cooperative training while maintaining semantic integrity and hidden security. However, its dependence on generative models and meticulously designed prompts raises apprehensions regarding scalability to real-time, varied communication requirements.

4. Discussion
4.1. Key Vulnerabilities and Threat Patterns in Generative SemCom
From the reviewed literature, several recurring themes emerge that collectively characterize the security landscape of Generative AI-assisted semantic communication (SemCom). A central issue concerns the use of shared semantic encoders. As demonstrated in [ 31], embedding-level adversarial attacks exploit widely shared encoders such as BERT, where minor perturbations can significantly degrade semantic fidelity. While shared encoders offer efficiency and interoperability, they simultaneously introduce a single point of failure. This observation highlights a fundamental trade-off between scalability and robustness in semantic system design.
Another prominent theme is knowledge-assisted semantic eavesdropping. Studies such as [ 28, 30] illustrate how adversaries can leverage shared knowledge bases, priors, or contextual alignment mechanisms to infer sensitive information from semantic representations. Unlike conventional data leakage, semantic-level exposure enables the inference of user intent and high-level meaning even when surface-level content remains protected.
The emergence of concealed semantic triggers introduces an additional and particularly insidious threat vector. The covert semantic backdoor attack (CSBA) presented in [ 12] demonstrates how malicious manipulations can remain dormant and undetectable until activated under specific semantic conditions, especially in intelligent vehicle environments. Such attacks expose the limitations of existing detection strategies, which often focus on distributional or signal-level anomalies while overlooking context-dependent semantic manipulations.
4.2. Limitations of Existing Defence Mechanisms
Robustness through model diversity represents a recurring defensive insight in the literature. The ROME framework in [ 32] shows that aggregating outputs from independently trained encoder–decoder models can enhance resilience against jamming and adversarial interference. Ensemble-based approaches reduce reliance on a single semantic pathway and introduce redundancy at the representation level. However, their deployment in distributed and resource-constrained environments raises practical concerns related to synchronization overhead, storage requirements, and coordination complexity.
Beyond individual defence mechanisms, a broader pattern emerges from resource-aware and optimization-focused studies. semantic security is frequently treated as an auxiliary constraint rather than a core architectural objective [ 24, 25, 26, 46, 50]. While metrics such as task accuracy, bandwidth efficiency, and energy consumption are commonly reported, adversarial robustness is often assumed or evaluated under simplified threat models.
A further concern arises from the imbalance between increasingly sophisticated attacks and comparatively limited defensive frameworks. Several attack-centric studies deliberately focus on exposing vulnerabilities related to backdoors, latent manipulation, and channel-triggered threats without proposing corresponding defences [ 14, 15, 16, 40]. Existing defensive approaches, including adversarial training and pruning, often remain tightly coupled to specific models or datasets, limiting their generalizability [ 13, 36].
4.3. Security Challenges Unique to Generative AI in SemCom
Generative AI introduces security concerns in semantic communication that differ from traditional signal-level or encoder-level risks. As noted by Xia et al. [ 6] and Ren et al. [ 4], GenAI-assisted semantic communication relies not only on transmitted semantic representations but also on generative priors, knowledge alignment, and model-driven reconstruction. This shifts the security problem from protecting transmitted symbols alone to securing the full semantic generation process.
A primary challenge is prompt injection and prompt manipulation. In the knowledge-base-enabled framework of Ren et al. [ 28], shared knowledge and generation guidance directly influence semantic reconstruction, which makes prompt-conditioned generation security-critical. Likewise, Du et al. [ 43] show that prompt-driven semantic communication depends heavily on secure prompt transmission and controlled generative recovery. These studies indicate that malicious prompt alteration can bias decoding, trigger unintended outputs, or expose sensitive information.
A second challenge is hallucination-induced semantic distortion. Ren et al. [ 28] explicitly identify hallucination and knowledge-base misuse as risks in generative semantic communication, while Ren et al. [ 4] discuss how GenAI-based reconstruction may produce plausible yet semantically unreliable outputs. In semantic communication, this creates a serious mismatch: the reconstructed result may appear coherent even when it no longer preserves the original task-relevant meaning.
A third challenge is diffusion model vulnerability. Grassucci et al. [ 29] show the growing importance of diffusion-based reconstruction in semantic communication, but later security-oriented studies reveal the associated risks more clearly. For example, Wang et al. [ 11] study model inversion in diffusion-aided task-oriented semantic communication, while Xi and Zhu [ 15] show that latent manipulation can distort semantic meaning through the diffusion process itself. Together, these works show that diffusion models improve reconstruction quality but also expand the semantic attack surface.
A fourth challenge is cross-modal security risk. Chen et al. [ 7] highlight the increasing role of cross-modal and generative semantic communication, where multiple modalities are semantically aligned during transmission and reconstruction. In parallel, Li et al. [ 19] and Nie et al. [ 20] demonstrate that multimodal foundation models remain vulnerable to backdoor and adversarial attacks that propagate across visual and textual reasoning pathways. This suggests that, in GenAI-assisted semantic communication, attacks may no longer remain confined to a single modality or representation space.
Overall, the surveyed literature indicates that Generative AI does not simply add new tools to semantic communication; it changes the nature of the security problem. As emphasized by Ren et al. [ 4], secure GenAI-SemCom must account for prompt manipulation, hallucinated semantic drift, diffusion-space vulnerabilities, and multimodal attack propagation in addition to conventional perturbation and eavesdropping threats.
4.4. Challenges in the Evaluation of Semantic Security
The surveyed literature indicates that evaluation remains one of the weakest aspects of secure semantic communication research. Classical communication metrics such as BER, PSNR, and MSE are still widely used because they quantify signal fidelity or reconstruction quality. However, Sagduyu et al. [ 3] show that semantic attacks can severely degrade task meaning even when conventional signal-level quality remains relatively stable. This reveals a fundamental gap between traditional communication evaluation and semantic security assessment.
This limitation is also evident in backdoor and generative settings. Xu et al. [ 12] report that covert semantic backdoors can remove or manipulate critical semantics while preserving perceptual quality measures such as PSNR. Similarly, Wang et al. [ 11] show that privacy and inversion risks can persist even when reconstruction quality remains high. These results make clear that acceptable BER, PSNR, or MSE does not by itself guarantee semantic correctness, task reliability, or privacy preservation.
The text-oriented studies in the survey therefore rely increasingly on meaning-aware metrics such as BLEU and BERTScore. For example, Hoang et al. [ 31] evaluate semantic degradation using BLEU and cosine similarity, while Peng et al. [ 36] use BLEU and BERTScore to assess robustness under semantic noise. These metrics are more informative than bit-level error rates, but they are still incomplete, since overlap-based or embedding-based similarity does not fully ensure preservation of task-relevant meaning under attack.
A further requirement is task-oriented evaluation. Wang et al. [ 41] explicitly connect security with semantic task reliability through the CLSSR framework, and Getu et al. [ 48] emphasize that goal-oriented semantic communication should be evaluated according to downstream task success rather than reconstruction fidelity alone. This is especially important in safety-critical systems, where a semantically plausible output is still unacceptable if it leads to the wrong decision or action.
Privacy and robustness evaluation also require dedicated metrics beyond semantic similarity alone. Tang et al. [ 9] study intelligent eavesdropping and privacy inference success, while He et al. [ 10] evaluate leakage reduction and eavesdropper performance under diffusion-enabled defence. These works show that privacy leakage, inversion success, attack success rate, and resilience across channel conditions are necessary parts of semantic security evaluation.
Overall, the survey and positioning works consistently point to the absence of a unified evaluation framework. Won et al. [ 24], Meng et al. [ 25], and Guo et al. [ 26] all note the lack of standardized semantic-security metrics and common benchmarks. As a result, the field still lacks a consistent methodology that jointly captures semantic similarity, task success, privacy leakage, and robustness under realistic communication conditions.
4.5. Open Challenges and Research Gaps
Despite significant progress in semantic communication research, the surveyed literature reveals several persistent challenges that remain insufficiently addressed. A primary gap lies in the architectural dependence on shared semantic encoders, which, while enabling interoperability and efficiency, introduce centralized vulnerabilities that can be exploited through embedding-level and latent-space attacks [ 31].
Another unresolved challenge concerns the protection of semantic context in knowledge-assisted communication systems [ 28, 30]. Detection of context-dependent and covert semantic manipulations also remains an open problem, as demonstrated by CSBA-style attacks [ 12].
The adoption of Generative AI further amplifies these challenges. While generative models enhance semantic expressiveness and compression efficiency [ 4, 29], they also introduce vulnerabilities related to diffusion dynamics, latent amplification, and reasoning-driven manipulation [ 10, 11].
Finally, a broader structural limitation persists across the literature: semantic security is often evaluated in isolation, with limited consideration of cross-layer interactions and realistic threat assumptions [ 24, 25]. The absence of standardized benchmarks and unified semantic security metrics continues to hinder systematic comparison across studies.
Key Research Gaps:
Limited evaluation under realistic wireless channel conditions and deployment settings.
Lack of standardized semantic security metrics and unified benchmarking frameworks.
Minimal focus on GenAI-specific attacks, including prompt manipulation, diffusion-space perturbations, and agentic reasoning threats.
Insufficient multimodal and cross-layer security evaluation.
Fragmented defence design, with limited generalization across models, tasks, and modalities.
5. Conclusions and Future Directions
This survey examined the security landscape of generative semantic communication systems, with a particular focus on threats and defences operating at the semantic and generative layers. The reviewed literature demonstrates that semantic communication security has evolved from a transmission-centric concern to a system-level issue involving meaning integrity, inference reliability, and decision trustworthiness. Attacks targeting shared encoders, latent representations, knowledge bases, and generative reasoning mechanisms reveal that preserving signal fidelity alone is insufficient to guarantee semantic robustness.
The analysis further shows that while Generative AI enables expressive, adaptive, and resource-efficient semantic communication, it simultaneously introduces novel attack surfaces associated with latent manipulation, backdoor activation, diffusion dynamics, and cross-modal leakage. Existing defences remain fragmented, model-specific, and often reactive, lagging behind the rapid expansion of semantic-level threats. The absence of standardized security benchmarks, unified evaluation metrics, and cross-layer assessment frameworks continues to hinder systematic progress in this domain.
Overall, the findings underscore the necessity of treating semantic security as a foundational design principle rather than a secondary optimization objective. Advancing secure generative semantic communication will require architectures that jointly consider semantic extraction, generative reconstruction, and task execution under adversarial conditions, ensuring that future semantic communication systems are not only efficient and intelligent, but also fundamentally trustworthy. Recent studies on secure digital twins for electric vehicles demonstrate that model-aware cyber attacks and evolutionary optimization–based defences can significantly affect cyber–physical decision pipelines, underscoring the importance of extending semantic-security analysis beyond communication blocks to system-level digital twins [ 51, 52, 53].
Future research should focus on developing standardized semantic-security benchmarks and unified evaluation metrics that jointly capture semantic fidelity, privacy leakage, and task reliability under adversarial conditions [ 24, 25, 26]. Another important direction is the design of defence mechanisms for multimodal and GenAI-native threats, including prompt manipulation, diffusion-space attacks, agentic reasoning attacks, and backdoors in LLM/VLM-based semantic communication systems [ 4, 5, 19, 20]. In addition, future work should investigate cross-layer and real-world evaluation frameworks that account for wireless channel dynamics, shared knowledge bases, and system-level deployment settings such as intelligent vehicles, edge intelligence, and digital twins [ 27, 30, 53]. Finally, lightweight and adaptive security mechanisms are needed to balance robustness, latency, energy efficiency, and semantic performance in practical next-generation semantic communication networks [ 22, 23, 24].
